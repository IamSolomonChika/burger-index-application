What this is

I looked at the US burger delivery market across 20 metro areas, 4 delivery platforms, and 9 cuisine types. This is the kind of work Burger Index does for Kitopi, Allo Beirut, and Nestlé in the 9 countries you cover today. I wanted to see what it looks like applied to the US, since that's the next market on the roadmap.

New York City leads

Score: 81.3 out of 100. Largest metro (20.1M people), 68% delivery penetration, $29.91 average basket. The obvious place to start.

Delivery adds 39% to a burger

On average, across all 20 cities. Fees and service charges push a $16 menu burger to $23 at checkout. Chicago is worst, at 42%.

Burger inflation is running hot

About 5% year over year, versus 3.8% for general food-away-from-home (BLS CPI). The gap has been widening since 2019. Restaurants are getting squeezed.

The Big Mac gap

A Big Mac costs $6.12 at the counter (2026). The average delivered burger runs $22.68. People pay nearly four times as much for the convenience of delivery.

How I built this

Everything in this report runs from a single Python script. No manual steps between raw data and the final chart.

1

Data sources

Three primary sources. The Economist's Big Mac Index (1,948 observations, 60+ countries, 2000-2026, MIT licensed on GitHub). BLS Food Away from Home CPI via FRED (series CUUR0000SEFV, monthly since 1953). Census/ACS demographics for 20 metros. Delivery platform data is simulated from publicly reported fee structures for DoorDash, Uber Eats, Grubhub, and Postmates. In production, that last piece would be replaced by live scraping.

2

Cleaning and normalization

I aligned the quarterly Big Mac data to the monthly BLS series by forward-filling. Demographics joined on metro name. For the delivery data, I normalized item price, delivery fee, and service fee into a single total basket column, then flagged outliers with IQR per city-platform-cuisine group.

3

Derived metrics

Delivery markup percentage: how much the total basket exceeds the menu price. Fee burden: delivery plus service fee as a share of menu price. A composite market opportunity score blending five normalized factors. Burger-specific cuts were pulled out separately. Inflation is indexed to 2019 = 100.

4

Visualization

Ten charts at 300 DPI via Matplotlib and Seaborn: time series, ranked bars, bubble plots, heatmaps, platform comparisons. All saved to output/charts/ with descriptive filenames.

5

Reproducibility

One command: python generate_charts.py. Pinned dependencies in requirements.txt. Raw data in data/, processed intermediates alongside it.

What I'd build for the US launch

Start with NYC, LA, SF

38 million people combined, 69% average delivery penetration, $29.78 average basket. The revenue density per square mile is higher here than anywhere else in the country.

Then Chicago, DC, Seattle

Another 20 million people, 62% penetration. Chicago's 42% markup means consumers there pay the most for delivery, which means restaurants there have the strongest reason to buy pricing intelligence.

Burgers are the wedge

Burger markup runs 35 to 42% versus 28 to 35% for pizza. Shake Shack, Five Guys, and the wave of smash burger concepts opening across the US have the most to gain from seeing real-time prices.

The missing piece is live data

This report uses simulated snapshots. A production version means scraping menus, limited-time offers, and dynamic delivery fees continuously. The model and scoring are ready. The ingestion layer is what I'd build first.

Five things to ship first

  1. A daily price tracker for the top 50 burger chains across four platforms in ten metros
  2. A fee transparency dashboard that breaks down delivery and service fees by platform, city, and cuisine
  3. Inflation alerts that fire when burger price growth exceeds BLS food-away CPI by more than 1.5 percentage points
  4. A reusable expansion score for any new metro, fed by Census and platform APIs
  5. Competitive benchmarking for Shake Shack versus Five Guys versus local concepts, updated daily
